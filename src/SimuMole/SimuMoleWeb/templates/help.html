Help


PDB file

Protein Data Bank (PDB) file is a textual file that describes the three-dimensional structures of the molecules by storing ATOM records (which represent atomic coordinates, occupancy, temperature factor and more) for each atom in the molecule.
You can find PDB files describing a variety of molecules here and you can also generate your own PDB file (note that this includes learning the mechanics of a PDB file or using some external tool).


DCD file

DCD trajectory file is a binary file that contains the atomic coordinates of the atoms for every timestep of the simulation.
After running a simulation via our Create Simulation tab you can download the DCD file and use it later either in our website via the Upload PDB & DCD tab or via other programs (like PyMOL) as well.


How to create a simulation?

1. Click on the Create Simulation tab.
2. Upload 1 or 2 PDB files according to your choice. You can choose the PDB files by their associated id or from your computer.
3. Click Next and wait for our check system to approve your PDBs / IDs.
4. If you uploaded 2 PDB files in step 2, determine the relative position between them by entering for each protein the amount of distance and the degrees to rotate from its initial location.
  4.1. Click Next and wait for our check system to verify that the molecules are not overlapping.
5. Enter temperature (Kelvin / Celsius) and the number of steps of the simulation.
6. Click Submit and wait for the simulation creation.
7. Examine your simulation. See Examine your simulation section for more details.


How to view a simulation animation using existing PDB and DCD files?

1. Click on the Upload PDB & DCD tab.
2. Click on Choose File, navigate to your PDB / DCD file and open it. Do it for the PDB file and its related DCD file.
3. Click on Upload and wait for the animation creation.
4. Examine your simulation. See Examine your simulation section for more details.


Examine your simulation

After creating or uploading a simulation (as explained in the previous sections), the Examine Your Simulation page will show up.
In the Examine Your Simulation page, you can view the animation of your simulation from 10 different angles.
To start running the animation, click on the Play button. To pause the animation, click on the Pause button.
To restart the animation click on the Restart button. To move forward or backward click on the +5 frames or -5 frames buttons, respectively.
To play an animation on the big screen, click on the animation.
The Full Control button will provide you some control buttons for each animation separately.


Download the output files

After creating or uploading a simulation (as explained in the previous sections), the Examine Your Simulation page will show up.
At the bottom of the page, you will find the download files option. Follow these steps to download your files as Zip:
1. Select the files you want to download. You can download the PDB file, the DCD file, and the animation files.
2. Choose how to download the files:
    Option 1: Direct download.
    Option 2: Via email (you will have to enter your email). Notice that due to the limitation of the size of files that can be sent via email, you may get a link to your desired files.


How to view your proteins in the PyMOL program?

1. Make sure you have PyMOL installed on your computer. If not, you can download it from here and then follow the instructions to install it.
2. After you open PyMOL, click the file tab and then click the open option.
3. In the open window, navigate to your PDB file and open it. You will see the proteins that the PDB file represents.


How to view your simulation in the PyMOL program?

1. Do the steps in the previous section to open the PDB file.
2. Repeat 1. with the DCD file to open the related DCD file. In the popped-up window, click on load.
3. click on the play button in the right bottom corner.


Note that our capability of setting the relative position between molecules is limited, so we encourage you to upload 1 PDB file that contains the merged molecules.
This can be done using the PyMOL program.
